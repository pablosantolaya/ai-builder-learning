Economic Costs and Opportunities of Climate Transition
International Economic Forum — Research Brief, Q1 2025

The economic cost of inaction on climate change now exceeds the cost of action by a ratio of approximately 6:1 according to updated modeling by the International Monetary Fund. Unmitigated climate change would reduce global GDP by 18–23% by 2100 through crop failures, infrastructure damage, productivity losses in heat-affected regions, and increased healthcare burdens. By contrast, achieving net-zero emissions by 2050 would require cumulative investment of approximately $125 trillion — equivalent to roughly 1.4% of global GDP per year through mid-century.

The transition creates significant employment disruption. The fossil fuel sector directly employs 10 million people globally, with an estimated 40 million more in adjacent industries. These jobs are concentrated in specific geographies — coal mining communities in Appalachia, the Ruhr Valley, and Inner Mongolia; oil and gas workers in the Gulf States, Alberta, and the North Sea. Transition planning that fails to account for these communities risks deepening inequality and generating political backlash that could slow the transition itself.

At the same time, clean energy employment is growing rapidly. The renewable energy sector employed 13.7 million people in 2023, up from 7.3 million in 2012. The IEA projects that a net-zero pathway would create 30 million new clean energy jobs by 2030, more than offsetting losses in fossil fuels — but these jobs are not necessarily in the same locations or accessible to the same workers without significant retraining investment.

Emerging economies face a particularly acute tension. Nations like India, Indonesia, and Nigeria require massive increases in energy consumption to lift populations out of poverty, and fossil fuels remain the cheapest way to provide that energy at scale in the short term. The argument that developed nations — which built their wealth on cheap fossil fuels — should bear the cost of enabling clean energy transitions in developing economies has become a central point of contention in international climate negotiations.

Green finance mechanisms including carbon markets, green bonds, and climate transition funds have mobilized an estimated $850 billion in 2024, but independent analysts estimate that $4–5 trillion per year is required to fund a Paris-aligned transition. Closing this gap will require both policy reform and institutional innovation in how climate investment is structured and incentivized across public and private sectors.
