Policy Pathways to Net Zero: A Comparative Analysis
Climate Policy Institute — Policy Brief, February 2025

Three broad policy models have emerged as the leading frameworks for national climate strategy. Their relative effectiveness is actively debated, and no single approach has achieved the scale of emissions reductions required. Understanding their trade-offs is essential for policymakers navigating the constraints of democratic accountability, market dynamics, and international competition.

Carbon pricing — through either carbon taxes or cap-and-trade systems — is the approach favored by most mainstream economists. By putting a direct price on emissions, it lets markets find the lowest-cost abatement opportunities and generates revenue that can fund further transition or compensate affected workers. British Columbia's carbon tax, Sweden's carbon levy (now at $130/tonne), and the EU Emissions Trading System are the most-studied examples. Critics argue that carbon prices high enough to drive real change are politically unsustainable in most democracies, and that in the absence of border carbon adjustments, they can push emissions to jurisdictions with weaker regulations rather than actually reducing them globally.

Command-and-control regulation — setting mandatory emissions standards for vehicles, appliances, buildings, and industries — has a strong track record of driving specific technological transitions without requiring a broad political consensus on carbon pricing. California's vehicle emissions standards effectively drove EV adoption in the United States, and EU appliance efficiency standards have delivered measurable energy savings. The limitation is that standards are sector-specific and tend to be set conservatively to maintain industry feasibility, meaning they rarely achieve the fastest possible transition.

Industrial policy — direct government investment in and support for clean energy industries — has gained significant momentum following the US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 and similar programs in the EU and China. The IRA's $369 billion in clean energy incentives triggered $3 trillion in private investment commitments within two years, accelerating domestic clean energy manufacturing. The risk is that subsidy competition between major economies can become economically inefficient and politically destabilizing, and that poorly targeted subsidies may fund technologies or companies that would have succeeded without support.

The evidence suggests that these approaches are most effective in combination. Carbon pricing drives economy-wide incentives; sector-specific standards ensure minimum performance floors; industrial policy accelerates the scaling of nascent technologies. Countries that have made the most progress — Denmark, Germany, the UK — have used all three tools.

One area of emerging consensus is the importance of social policy accompanying climate policy. The "yellow vest" protests in France following fuel tax increases demonstrated that climate measures perceived as regressive — burdening lower-income households more than wealthy ones — generate intense political opposition. Effective climate policy must be designed with distributional impacts explicitly in mind, either through revenue recycling, targeted support for affected communities, or preferential pricing for essential energy use.
