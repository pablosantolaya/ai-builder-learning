Climate Change: Current Status and Projections
Global Climate Research Institute — Annual Review 2025

Global average temperatures have risen by 1.2°C above pre-industrial levels as of 2024, placing the world on a trajectory that exceeds the 1.5°C threshold targeted by the Paris Agreement within the next decade. Arctic ice coverage has declined 40% since 1980, accelerating sea-level rise at a rate of 3.6 mm per year.

The primary driver of warming remains the burning of fossil fuels, which currently account for 73% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Industrial agriculture adds another 18%, primarily through methane from livestock and nitrous oxide from fertilizers. Deforestation contributes the remaining 9%.

Extreme weather events have increased dramatically in frequency and intensity. The period 2015–2024 saw more category 4 and 5 hurricanes than the entire previous four decades combined. Wildfire seasons in North America, Australia, and Southern Europe now extend year-round rather than being seasonal. Drought conditions affecting food production have become endemic in Sub-Saharan Africa and Central Asia.

Climate models now project that without significant intervention, warming of 2.8–3.1°C above pre-industrial levels is locked in by 2100. This would displace an estimated 1.2 billion people from coastal and arid regions, reduce global agricultural yields by 25–40%, and trigger cascading ecosystem collapse in tropical and boreal zones.

However, there is cautious optimism. Renewable energy deployment has accelerated beyond initial projections. Solar capacity doubled globally between 2022 and 2024, and wind capacity grew 35% in the same period. If current growth rates continue, renewable sources could supply 60% of global electricity by 2035. The critical gap remains transportation, heavy industry, and heating — sectors that collectively account for 55% of emissions and have seen slower decarbonization progress.

The scientific consensus is clear: limiting warming to 2°C requires a 43% reduction in global emissions by 2030. Current pledges under the Paris Agreement, if fully implemented, would achieve only a 7% reduction. The gap between pledges and required action remains the central challenge of global climate policy.
